## The Origin: Howard vs. Leila

The concept comes from Buffett's own family. His father, Howard Buffett, was a "100% inner scorecard guy" — a maverick who "just didn't care about what other people thought." Howard became a stockbroker in 1927, two years before the market collapsed, and went four months without a commission. His first sale earned $5. He didn't waver. He also drilled into his children a maxim from Emerson: *"The great man is he who in the middle of the crowd keeps with perfect sweetness the independence of solitude."* [ep-0182]

His mother Leila was the opposite. As the transcript from ep-0182 puts it: *"She would turn on Warren and his sisters with the wrath of God... She scolded and degraded her children. Nothing they had done measured up. She compared, criticized and dredged up every imaginable failing."* She was obsessed with how the family appeared to others — the outer scorecard in its most destructive form. Buffett had a terrible relationship with her and an exceptionally close one with his father. The lesson wasn't abstract; it was wired into his childhood.

Senra captures the core definition from Buffett directly [ep-0100]:

> *"The big question about how people behave is whether they've got an inner scorecard or an outer scorecard. It helps if you can be satisfied with an inner scorecard, I would also say it's probably the single only way to have a happy life."*

And then the diagnostic metaphor Buffett uses: *"If the world couldn't see your results, would you rather be thought of as the world's greatest investor but in reality have the world's worst record, or be thought of as the world's worst investor when you actually have the best?"* That's the acid test.

## How It Shows Up in Buffett's Decisions

The inner scorecard isn't just a philosophy — it explains specific, concrete choices Buffett made that looked strange to outsiders:

**Secrecy.** Buffett rejected the transparency of his mentor Ben Graham, who shared holdings openly. He demanded complete secrecy for his partnership: investors got an annual summary and could only add or withdraw money on December 31. He wasn't going to let anyone's opinion or imitation affect his decisions [ep-0100].

**Being misunderstood.** When Berkshire bought an insurance company while still notionally a textile mill, observers couldn't fathom it. Buffett didn't care: *"Buffett did not think of Berkshire as necessarily a textile company, but as a corporation whose capital ought to be deployed in the greenest possible pasture."* [ep-0182] He let the company be misunderstood.

**Developing eccentricities early.** From the shareholder letters [ep-0088], Buffett quotes David Ogilvy: *"Develop your eccentricities while you're young. That way when you get old, people won't think that you're going gaga."* Build a reputation for unconventional behavior so that later, nobody bothers you about it. This is a social hack in service of the inner scorecard — insulating yourself from the pressure to conform before the pressure even arrives.

**Self-belief before the record.** As a young man running partnerships from his bedroom with no track record, Buffett was already worrying about how to handle the fortune he was certain he'd accumulate. *"At a time when his accomplishments were modest, Buffett's awesome self-confidence was the thing that propelled him. He had no record as an independent operator. He had nothing on paper to indicate that he was worthy of people's trust."* [ep-0182] The inner scorecard means the belief precedes the evidence.

The outer scorecard crept in and made him miserable, even amid objective success.

**Bill Walsh (ep-0106):** Walsh's complementary warning: after success, the media called him a "genius" and he was *"naive enough to be flattered."* He eventually realized that accepting such labels corrupts your internal compass and makes you dependent on outside validation. *"Don't let anybody call you a genius."* This is the outer scorecard in disguise — it doesn't always come as criticism; sometimes it comes as praise.

## The Tension: Reputation Still Matters

Here's where it gets interesting and non-obvious. The vault contains a productive tension: Buffett and Munger both clearly believe the inner scorecard is essential, but they also treat *reputation* as a genuine asset.

Charlie Munger's view, captured in ep-0090: *"reputation is persuasive."* He repeats ideas in different ways because he understands that reputation carries weight logic alone cannot. Jeff Bezos arrives at the same conclusion independently in ep-0071: reputation fills the gaps when memory is fallible.

This isn't a contradiction. The inner scorecard governs *how you make decisions*. Reputation is a *second-order consequence* of making good decisions consistently. The key distinction: you don't make decisions *in order to* build reputation. You make decisions based on your own standards, and reputation accrues as a side effect. Campeau inverted this — he wanted the reputation without the standards, so he manufactured parties no one attended.

And Buffett himself complicates the picture with the deepest measure of the inner scorecard [ep-0100]:

> *"When you get to my age, you'll really measure your success in life by how many of the people you want to have love you actually do love you. I know people who have a lot of money, and they get testimonial dinners and they get hospital wings named after them."*

This redefines the inner scorecard entirely. It's not about ignoring other people. It's about *choosing whose opinion counts*. The inner scorecard isn't solipsism — it's selective, deliberate weighting. Your father's respect matters. The genuine love of people you actually care about matters. The noise of strangers and headlines doesn't.
