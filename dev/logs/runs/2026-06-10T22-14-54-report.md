> How did Edison and Rockefeller differ in how they built teams?

**Edison built a workshop around himself. Rockefeller built an organization that could run without him.** That's the core difference, and it flows directly from what each man was actually trying to do.

Edison's primary drive was autonomy — the freedom to tinker. His "one ambition" was "to be able to work without regard to the expense... What I want is the perfect workshop" [ep-0083]. He hired assistants to extend his own hands: people like Batchelor who handled correspondence so he could stay in the lab, and a rotating cast of young men who worked alongside him in the noisy shops [ep-0003]. But it was always *his* shop, *his* inventions, *his* celebrity. He valued hustle and action — "I like a hustler" — and seems to have selected people who matched his intensity rather than complemented his weaknesses [ep-0003]. The evidence on his specific hiring practices is admittedly thin — the vault focuses far more on Edison's personal traits than his team-building philosophy — but the pattern is clear: he built a creative laboratory centered on himself.

Rockefeller, by contrast, built a machine designed to outlast any single person. He "hired talented people as found, not as needed" — a proactive, almost anticipatory approach to talent acquisition [ep-0016]. His genius move was absorbing adversaries: after the Cleveland Massacre, "he winds up getting a lot of these people to work for them in Standard Oil" — turning former enemies into lieutenants [ep-0016]. He then delegated aggressively, "dedicated most of his time to broad policy decisions" while partners like Henry Flagler ran operations [ep-0016]. His operating philosophy was partnership language: "Never forget that we are all partners. Whatever is done for the general good is done for the good of us all" [ep-0148]. He was the strategist; the system was the product.

The contrast is really about what each man was optimizing for. Edison optimized for creative freedom — the team existed to serve his inventive process. Rockefeller optimized for durable market dominance — the team *was* the competitive advantage. One was a soloist with accompanists; the other was a conductor who built an orchestra capable of playing without him.
