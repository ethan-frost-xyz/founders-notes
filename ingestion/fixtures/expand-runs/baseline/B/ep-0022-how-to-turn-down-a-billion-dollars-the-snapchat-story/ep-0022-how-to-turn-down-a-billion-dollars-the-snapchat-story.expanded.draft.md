### 22:03 — The Idea of “Standard” Is a Construct

Context: After Lightspeed Venture Partners included terms in Snapchat’s first funding round that gave them control over future financing, Evan Spiegel felt betrayed. He later warned other entrepreneurs about blindly accepting “standard” terms, recounting the experience in a 2013 Stanford keynote.

Quote: “When I asked a question because I didn't understand something, I was reassured that the term was standard and, therefore, agreeable. **I forgot that the idea of standard is a construct. It simply does not exist.** So rather than attempt to further understand the document, I accepted it.” (22:03)

Key takeaway: The label “standard” is often used to deflect scrutiny and pressure founders into accepting unfavorable terms. Spiegel’s mistake illustrates that entrepreneurs must personally understand every clause in a deal, because what is presented as industry norm may be anything but, and the cost of blind trust can be severe.

### 1:00:05 — Privacy Is the Understanding of Context

Context: In an April 2014 keynote, Evan Spiegel laid out his philosophy on privacy versus secrecy, drawing on the work of scholar Helen Nissenbaum. He argued that digital platforms misunderstand privacy by treating it as hiding information rather than as a contextual practice.

Quote: “Unfortunately, privacy is too often articulated as secrecy. **When as Nissenbaum points out, privacy is actually focused on an understanding of context, not what is said, but where it is said and to whom.** Privacy allows us to enjoy and learn from the intimacy that is created when we share different things with different people and different contexts.” (1:00:05)

Key takeaway: Real privacy isn’t about concealing facts; it’s about controlling the context in which we share. This insight reveals why one-to-many social platforms feel invasive—they collapse distinct social contexts into a single audience, eroding the natural human behavior of varying self-presentation.

### 1:02:29 — Social Media Is Capitalism Entering Our Relationships

Context: Continuing his 2014 keynote, Spiegel connected the design of social media to market dynamics, arguing that platforms turn personal expression into a popularity contest that forces users to perform a consistent, brand-like identity.

Quote: “Popular expression becomes the most valuable expression. **Social media businesses represent an aggressive expansion of capitalism into our personal relationships.** We are asked to perform for our friends, to create things they like, to work on a personal brand.” (1:02:29)

Key takeaway: By quantifying feelings through likes and shares, social media commodifies human connection and pressures people to curate a marketable self. Spiegel’s critique highlights a structural tension: platforms profit from engagement, but that incentive warps authentic relationships into transactional performances.
