### 08:07 — Keeping costs low

Context: Senra introduces Ford’s fanatical focus on costs, emphasizing that wages are not a cost to be cut. He is summarizing Ford’s philosophy before diving into the “wage motive” later in the book.

Quote: "He has this thing called the wage motive, which I'll get to in a little bit. Key takeaway: Ford treated cost‑cutting as a discipline that must never touch wages; low costs and high wages could coexist only through relentless, intelligent elimination of waste. For a founder, this means scrutinising every expense except those that directly sustain the people who create value. The lesson is that cost discipline should strengthen, not weaken, the organisation’s ability to serve and grow its own customer base.

### 30:21 — Wasted time does not litter the floor like wasted material

Context: Ford dedicates a chapter to the meaning of time, asserting that time waste is the most insidious form of waste because it leaves no visible trace. Senra quotes this passage as a warning.

Quote: "Time waste differs from material waste in that there can be no salvage. The easiest of all waste and the hardest to correct is the waste of time because **wasted time does not litter the floor like wasted material**. In our industries, we think of time as human energy." (30:21)

Key takeaway: Unlike scrap metal, lost time cannot be recovered or sold; it simply evaporates. ### 32:00 — Wage motive

Context: Senra introduces Ford’s central thesis: that high wages combined with low prices create a virtuous cycle of demand. This principle is the foundation of Ford’s approach to business.

Quote: "High wages with high prices do not help anyone. It just means that everything else has been marked up. But **higher wages and low prices means greater buying power, which means more customers**. The payment of high wages, however, is not just a matter of wishing to pay them." (32:00)

Key takeaway: Ford rejected the zero‑sum view that wages and profits are in conflict. By paying workers enough to afford the products they made, he expanded his own market. The wage motive teaches that a business can be engineered so that every stakeholder—worker, customer, and owner—prospers together, but that requires a deliberately structured economic model.

### 35:00 — Four principles of management

Context: Ford distills his management philosophy into three (or four) crisp directives. Senra notes that these principles are simple enough to apply to any business.

Quote: "And now we have Ford's principles on management or of management rather. The **four principles of management** are extremely simple. They may be compressed into three statements. Number one, do the job in the most direct fashion without bothering with red tape or any of the ordinary divisions of authority. Two, pay every man well, not less than $6 a day and see that he's employed all the time, do 48 hours a week and no longer. Key takeaway: Ford’s principles strip management to its essentials: simplify workflow, reward generously, and maintain an environment that commands respect. By avoiding bureaucratic layering and focusing on direct action, he created a culture where productivity and dignity reinforced each other. Founders can use these rules as a litmus test—when complexity creeps in, return to these first principles.
