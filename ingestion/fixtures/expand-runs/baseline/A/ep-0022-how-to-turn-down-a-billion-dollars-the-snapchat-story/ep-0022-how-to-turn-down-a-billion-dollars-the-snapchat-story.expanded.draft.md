### 22:03 — The Idea of “Standard” Is a Construct

Context: Evan Spiegel describes his early mistake with Snapchat’s first venture term sheet. A fund had told him certain terms were “standard,” and he accepted them without fully understanding the implications, later realizing they gave away valuable rights and made fundraising harder. He warns other founders not to trust the word “standard.”

Quote: “When I asked a question because I didn't understand something, I was reassured that the term was standard and, therefore, agreeable. **I forgot that the idea of standard is a construct. It simply does not exist.** So rather than attempt to further understand the document, I accepted it.” (22:03)

Key takeaway: “Standard” is not an objective fact but a negotiating tactic that protects the party proposing it. Founders who hear that a term is “standard” should assume it is likely not in their favor and must dig into the actual mechanics of the clause themselves. Delegating that responsibility to lawyers or investors can lead to an expensive loss of control down the line.

### 1:00:05 — Privacy Is the Understanding of Context

Context: In a 2014 keynote, Evan Spiegel distinguishes privacy from secrecy by citing privacy scholar Helen Nissenbaum. He argues that privacy is about respecting the context in which something is shared—who you are speaking to and where—rather than simply hiding information.

Quote: “Unfortunately, privacy is too often articulated as secrecy. **When as Nissenbaum points out, privacy is actually focused on an understanding of context, not what is said, but where it is said and to whom.** Privacy allows us to enjoy and learn from the intimacy that is created when we share different things with different people and different contexts.” (1:00:05)

Key takeaway: Real privacy is not just about keeping secrets; it is about maintaining the situational boundaries that make relationships meaningful. Social apps that flatten all audiences into one public feed erase context, breaking the natural human pattern of sharing different selves with different groups. Designing for context encourages more honest, intimate communication.

### 1:02:29 — Social Media Is the Result of Capitalism Getting into Our Relationships

Context: Continuing his critique of modern social platforms, Evan Spiegel argues that the economics of social media force users to perform for likes and build a consistent personal brand. This replaces authentic, contradictory human expression with a polished identity designed to attract engagement.

Quote: “Popular expression becomes the most valuable expression. **Social media businesses represent an aggressive expansion of capitalism into our personal relationships.** We are asked to perform for our friends, to create things they like, to work on a personal brand.” (1:02:29)

Key takeaway: When social networks monetize attention, they incentivize users to treat their identities as products, optimizing for mass approval rather than authentic connection. This mirrors a market logic inside friendship, pushing people toward a single, consistent persona that eliminates the natural contradictions of being human. Recognizing this collapse of private life into public performance is the first step toward building tools that restore genuine social interaction.
