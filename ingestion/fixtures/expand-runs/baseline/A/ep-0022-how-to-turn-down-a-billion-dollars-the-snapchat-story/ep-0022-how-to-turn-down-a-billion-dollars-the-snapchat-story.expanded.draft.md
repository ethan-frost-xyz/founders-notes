### 22:03 — The idea of “standard” is a construct in venture term sheets

Context: Evan Spiegel reflects on his first term sheet from Jeremy Liew and Lightspeed, where he was told certain terms were “standard” and therefore agreeable. He later realized this acceptance was a costly mistake after the company grew and needed more capital.

Quote: "When I asked a question because I didn’t understand something, I was reassured that the term was standard and, therefore, agreeable. **I forgot that the idea of standard is a construct. It simply does not exist.** So rather than attempt to further understand the document, I accepted it." (22:03)

Key takeaway: Founders should never accept “standard” as a justification without fully parsing every term themselves; what is labeled standard often masks hidden traps that can severely constrain future fundraising or control. This lesson emphasizes the need to question authority in high-stakes negotiations and to invest the time to truly understand agreements, as the cost of deference can be enormous.

### 1:00:05 — Privacy is the understanding of context, not secrecy

Context: Evan explains his philosophy on privacy during a keynote, drawing on professor Helen Nissenbaum’s work. This moment underscores Snapchat’s design thesis: digital communication should respect the contextual boundaries humans naturally use in person.

Quote: "Unfortunately, privacy is too often articulated as secrecy. When as Nissenbaum points out, **privacy is actually focused on an understanding of context, not what is said, but where it is said and to whom.** Privacy allows us to enjoy and learn from the intimacy that is created when we share different things with different people and different contexts." (1:00:05)

Key takeaway: Real privacy isn’t about hiding everything; it’s about preserving the appropriate context so we can be ourselves with different groups. For product builders, this reframes features around enabling authentic, context-specific sharing rather than forcing a single public-facing identity, which ultimately deepens user trust and engagement.

### 1:02:29 — Social media is capitalism entering our personal relationships

Context: Evan criticizes how social platforms quantify expression through popularity, mirroring market dynamics. He argues this turns personal interactions into commodified performances and pressures users to adopt a consistent “brand” across all audiences.

Quote: "Popular expression becomes the most valuable expression. **Social media businesses represent an aggressive expansion of capitalism into our personal relationships.** We are asked to perform for our friends, to create things they like, to work on a personal brand." (1:02:29)

Key takeaway: When platforms incentivize engagement metrics over genuine connection, they reshape friendships into transactional exchanges where authenticity suffers. Founders should examine whether their product design inadvertently pushes users toward performance, and instead explore models that honor the messy, contradictory nature of human relationships.
