### 42:00 — Alfred Nobel’s belief that dynamite would end all wars

Context: Nobel explains to a friend his hope that increasingly devastating weapons will make war unthinkable. He echoes a recurring historical delusion shared by Sam Colt and atomic scientists.

Quote: "He told her that the art of war was just beginning. Only when it had reached its completion would the deterrent elements be such that all nations would be forced to live in peace. On another occasion, Alfred expressed something similar: 'I would like to invent a substance or a machine **so frightfully effective and devastating that it would forever make wars altogether impossible.** ' He thought the invention of dynamite, the fact that it was so good at killing massive amounts of people so rapidly would lead to eternal peace." (42:00)

Key takeaway: Inventors of destructive technologies often overestimate the deterrent effect of their creations, assuming rationality will override human aggression. This pattern—from the revolver to the atom bomb—reveals a persistent blind spot: technology alone cannot extinguish the will to fight, and betting on mutual fear tends to backfire.

### 51:00 — Never try to do everything yourself

Context: Alfred Nobel advises his brother Ludvig, who is running a massive oil company and sinking under the weight of micromanagement. Alfred is urging Ludvig to delegate and conserve his energy.

Quote: "Never do yourself what others could do better or equally well. **Anyone who tries to do everything himself would become worn out of body and soul.** " (51:00)

Key takeaway: As a business scales, founder-level control must give way to specialized partners who handle what the leader cannot. Nobel’s advice underscores a universal lesson: clinging to every task leads to burnout and limits growth, while letting go of non-essential work preserves the founder’s ability to focus on what only they can do.
