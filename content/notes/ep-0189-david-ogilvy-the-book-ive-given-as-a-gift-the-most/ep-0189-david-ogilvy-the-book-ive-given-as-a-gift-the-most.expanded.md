### 8:00 — Quality of salesmanship involves energy, time, and product knowledge

Context: In his 1935 sales manual for AGA Cooker salesmen, 24-year-old Ogilvy distills the essence of effective selling, moving beyond mere avoidance of boredom to the active ingredients of success.

Quote: "The worst fault a salesman can commit is to be a bore. **Quality of salesmanship** involves energy, time and knowledge of the product." (8:00)

Key takeaway: Ogilvy argues that sales excellence isn't passive; it demands deliberate investment of personal energy, dedicated time, and deep product expertise. This principle elevates sales from a mere transaction to a craft, applicable to any persuasive role.

### 9:00 — Use social proof and avoid naming competitors

Context: In the same sales manual, Ogilvy gives tactical advice on overcoming objections by leveraging satisfied customers and steering clear of negative comparisons.

Quote: "2 more bits of advice from this, “**use social proof and then avoid mentioning your competitors.**” And so he actually gives copy about examples of sales pitches, like actual terms that he would use to overcome specific objections." (9:00)

Key takeaway: Social proof builds trust by associating your offering with familiar, satisfied peers, while discussing competitors introduces a defensive, negative tone that can erode credibility. This lesson underscores the power of positive, third-party validation in any sales or marketing context.

### 17:00 — Opening line to job letter: “My father was in charge of the men’s lavatory at the Ritz”

Context: Ogilvy recalls the most memorable job application he ever received, from copywriter Ray Taylor, whose unconventional opening immediately set him apart.

Quote: "the first paragraph read. “**My father was in charge of the men's lavatory at the Ritz Hotel.** My mother was a chambermaid at the same hotel. I was educated at the London School of Economics.”" (17:00)

Key takeaway: A job application that opens with a vivid, personal, and unexpected story cuts through the noise of generic submissions. Ogilvy prized memorability and authenticity, showing that a human touch can make a candidate unforgettable and signal creative potential.

### 23:00 — People don’t like writing but like having written

Context: In a memo detailing his own laborious copywriting process, Ogilvy echoes a universal truth about creative work: the act is often painful, but the result brings satisfaction.

Quote: "If you’ll – you’ll hear them give talks or even write about the act of their craft of actually writing, a lot of them say that they **hate the act of writing, but they like having written.** And so there's kind of that theme that echoes in this memo where -- this letter that Ogilvy is outlining, he's going to outline, what is it, 12 -- it's going to be a little longer." (23:00)

Key takeaway: The struggle of creation is normal; the fulfillment comes from completion. Recognizing this can help creators push through resistance, knowing that the discomfort is temporary and the reward of having produced something worthwhile is lasting.

### 29:00 — Don’t be a bore, can’t save souls in an empty church

Context: Ogilvy repeatedly used this vivid maxim to stress that dull advertising fails to capture attention, making the message useless.

Quote: "This is one of his best maxims, one he repeats over and over again, “**don't be a dull bore. We can't save souls in an empty church.**” And again, that's just a lot more memorable than saying, hey, if your ads are boring, no one's going to read them." (29:00)

Key takeaway: In communication, being boring is the ultimate failure because it repels the audience. Ogilvy’s colorful language teaches that to persuade, you must first engage; otherwise, even the best message will be ignored.
