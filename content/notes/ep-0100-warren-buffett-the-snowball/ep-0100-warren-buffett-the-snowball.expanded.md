### 09:30 — Buffett considers himself a teacher first

Context: Early in the book, the author notes that while Buffett is passionate about investing, he views his primary role as a teacher, a trait he shared with Charlie Munger, and this drive shaped his prolific output of shareholder letters and public lessons.

Quote: "Something I also love about Warren, which I should have understood this because I read every single one of his shareholder letters. But first and foremost, like investing is his passion, but he considers himself a teacher first. And so does Charlie Munger, too." (09:30)

Key takeaway: Teaching is a positive‑sum activity that multiplies knowledge and leaves a lasting legacy. Buffett’s identity as a teacher before an investor explains why he devoted so much time to explaining his principles, enabling others to learn from his experience and avoid his mistakes.

### 12:00 — Inner scorecard vs outer scorecard

Context: Buffett learned the concept of inner versus outer scorecards from his father Howard, while his mother Leila exemplified the outer scorecard, leading to a life of worry and dissatisfaction.

Quote: "The big question about how people behave is whether they've got an inner scorecard or an outer scorecard. It helps if you can be satisfied with an inner scorecard, I would also say it's probably the single only way to have a happy life." (12:00)

Key takeaway: An inner scorecard means measuring yourself against your own standards rather than the approval of others. This mindset is essential for authentic success and personal happiness, because chasing external validation often leads to dissatisfaction and a life driven by others' opinions.

### 50:30 — Buffett’s insistence on secrecy for his investment partnership

Context: When raising money for his early investment partnership, Buffett rejected the transparency of his mentor Ben Graham, who shared his holdings openly. Instead, Buffett demanded complete control and secrecy to prevent others from copying his moves and diluting his returns.

Quote: "Not for him was Ben Graham's handicap of people riding on his co‑tail. Warren favored secrecy. He says they would get an annual summary of his performance, and they could put money in or withdraw it only on December 31." (50:30)

Key takeaway: Secrecy can be a strategic advantage in investing and business when it protects proprietary decision‑making. Buffett’s refusal to disclose his positions allowed him to build stakes without competition, illustrating how guarding your process can compound an edge over time.

### 55:00 — The cigar butt investing metaphor explained by Buffett

Context: Buffett describes his purchase of Berkshire Hathaway as a classic cigar‑butt investment—a cheap but struggling company that only offered a tiny residual value, a strategy he later regretted.

Quote: "So I bought my own cigar butt, and I tried to smoke it. This is Warren describing what that means: You walk down the street, and you see a cigar butt. And it's kind of soggy and disgusting and repels you, but it's free, and there may be one puff left in it." (55:00)

Key takeaway: The cigar‑butt approach seeks deep value in mediocre businesses, but Buffett learned that buying great companies at fair prices is far superior. The metaphor warns that a “free” puff can tie up time and capital that would be better deployed elsewhere, a lesson he took to heart when moving away from Graham’s pure deep‑value style.

### 1:20:30 — The 20‑punches card analogy for investing discipline

Context: Buffett taught students a mental model for investing: imagine a card with only 20 punches that represent all the financial decisions you can make in a lifetime, forcing extreme selectivity.

Quote: "To the students he explained his 20 punches approach to investing. He said, you'll get very rich if you thought of yourself as having a card with only 20 punches in a lifetime, and every financial decision used up one punch." (1:20:30)

Key takeaway: Artificial scarcity forces discipline, reducing the temptation to dabble and encouraging high‑conviction bets. This mental model can be applied beyond investing to any high‑stakes decision, reminding us that a few great choices outweigh many mediocre ones.
