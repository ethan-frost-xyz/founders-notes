### 05:00 — Always focus on the most valuable thing you can do at that point in time

Context: The speaker describes Aristotle Onassis’s chaotic travel and decision-making style. Onassis avoided rigid schedules, instead addressing the most pressing problem at any moment, which the speaker sees as a key lesson for entrepreneurs.

Quote: "But if he's pressed too hard about definitive dates and places, he is liable to plead, please do not pin me down. So the only thing I could say, he talk about that a lot in this book. And I think something I learned from him is the value and **always focusing on the most valuable thing you could be doing at that time.** So we didn't have a strict schedule." (05:00)

Key takeaway: Onassis’s method of constantly asking “what’s the most important problem I need to solve?” allowed him to deploy his attention with maximum impact. For founders, this means resisting the pull of preset calendars and instead prioritizing the single highest-leverage action available at any given moment. The discipline of continuous reprioritization is a hallmark of effective execution.

### 34:30 — Start with the problem and work backwards

Context: After detailing Onassis’s failed attempts to sell tobacco by knocking on doors, the speaker introduces the inversion principle. He explains that Onassis’s real breakthrough came when he abandoned the conventional sales approach and worked backwards from the desired outcome—getting the managing director to send him to the purchasing department. The same backwards logic later revolutionized shipping finance.

Quote: "Probably the most important thing you're going to learn from Aristotle Onassis is the same way I use another Charlie Munger quote, invert always invert and that when you have a problem to solve, which is basically what running a business is, you -- it makes sense in Charlie Munger and Warren Buffett's advice and then we're going to see this in Aristotle Onassis' actions is to **start at the problem and work backwards.** Most people don't do that." (34:30)

Key takeaway: Working backwards forces clarity: instead of starting with available resources and hoping they lead to a sale, Onassis identified the ultimate decision-maker and built a strategy around that constraint. This approach later let him secure tanker financing by first locking in oil-company charters and then presenting the guaranteed revenue stream to banks. For any entrepreneur, inverting the typical sequence—beginning with the end goal and engineering the steps backward—turns obstacles into solvable logistics.

This discipline is a common thread among self-made founders.
