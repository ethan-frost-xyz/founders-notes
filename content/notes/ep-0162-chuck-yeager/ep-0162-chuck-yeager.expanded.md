### 18:00 — Avoiding standard stupidities is a skill you can cultivate

Context: After observing that the worst pilots often died from their own mistakes, David Senra connects Chuck Yeager’s blunt assessment to Charlie Munger’s investment philosophy. Munger argues that systematically sidestepping common errors yields better results than chasing brilliance.

Quote: "And this is what Charlie said. ‘We’re talking about very simple ideas of just **figuring out the standard stupidities and avoiding them.** And I actually collect them,’ meaning he collects human stupidities. ‘Some people collect stamps. I collect insanities and absurdities, and then I avoid them. And it’s amazing how well it works because I’ve gone by the examples of all these people that are more talented than I am.’" (18:00)

Key takeaway: Actively cataloguing and avoiding typical blunders—in flying, business, or daily life—acts as a powerful protective edge. This practice turns humility into a durable advantage, allowing a person of ordinary ability to outperform more gifted peers simply by sidestepping the predictable ways people fail.

### 1:02:00 — Recognize that the good old days are happening now

Context: Looking back on the wild camaraderie at Pancho Barnes’s desert oasis, a friend’s memory prompts Senra to highlight a truth about nostalgia. The moments we live today will later be seen as the golden era, so they must be savored in the present.

Key takeaway: Time erodes the line between ordinary days and cherished memories; the periods we later romanticize are often the ones we are living right now. Treating the present with the same fondness we imagine for the past encourages a fuller, more grateful experience of life.

### 1:10:00 — Chuck Yeager’s simple formula: only work if it’s still fun

Context: After a long career of test flying and combat, Yeager boils his career decisions down to a single internal question. He had no patience for prestige or pay if the work ceased to delight him.

Quote: "The best way to fly safe was to know what in the hell you were doing. This is Chuck’s simple formula for work. **I use my own simple formula: either the Air Force was still fun for me or it wasn’t much fun anymore. If it wasn’t fun, why would I hang around?**" (1:10:00)

Key takeaway: Enjoyment is not a luxury but a reliable compass for sustaining excellence. When the joy drains out of a pursuit, the rational choice is to move on, preserving energy for endeavors where passion and skill still intersect.

### 1:16:00 — Refuse to say “I’m too old” and enjoy the years remaining

Context: Near the end of his autobiography, Yeager confronts the self-defeating habit of using age as an excuse to withdraw. He insists that the goal is not merely to live long but to keep engaging with what makes life worth living.

Quote: "You do what you can for as long as you can. And when you finally can’t, you do the next best thing: you back up, but you don’t give up. I know too many people who have erected barriers, real brick walls just because they have gray hair and prematurely cut themselves off from lifelong enjoyments by thinking, **‘I’m too old to do this or that. That’s for younger people.’** Living to a ripe old age is not an end in itself. The trick is to enjoy the years remaining." (1:16:00)

Key takeaway: Age becomes a prison only when people voluntarily lock the door. By refusing to define oneself by a birthdate and instead chasing continued curiosity and fun, a long life stays rich with purpose until the very end.
