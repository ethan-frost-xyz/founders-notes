## Expanded datapoints

### 10:00 — Exceptional Hours Drive 2–3x More Learning

Context: Claude Hopkins dismisses the idea that superior intelligence alone explains his advertising success, emphasizing that his intense work schedule—often until 2 a.m., with Sundays his best days—gave him more experience, mistakes, and break-throughs than peers.

Quote: "There is some difference in brains, of course, but it is not so important as the difference in industry. The man who does two or three times the work of another **learns two or three times as much.** He makes more mistakes and more successes, and he learns from both. If I had gone higher than others in advertising or done more, the fact is not due to exceptional ability, but to exceptional hours." (10:00)

Key takeaway: Putting in outsized hours compresses a lifetime of trial and error into a shorter period, accelerating learning because every success and every mistake becomes a teacher. Hopkins’s admission that this came at the cost of a balanced life underscores a deliberate trade‑off: deep mastery often demands exceptional, not merely efficient, investment of time.

### 22:00 — The Attitude of Mind Turns Work into Play

Context: While watching a railroad foreman who worked with enthusiasm—building his own house after a ten‑hour day—Hopkins heard a lesson that reframed his entire approach to advertising. The foreman contrasted his own game‑like devotion with the drudgery of his crew, who loafed and killed time.

Quote: "In both, there is a rivalry. There's a struggle to excel the rest. **All the difference I see lies in the attitude of mind.**" (22:00)

Key takeaway: The foreman’s insight is that work and play are interchangeable when approached with the same competitive spirit and curiosity. For Hopkins, this meant cultivating a love of work so deep that he preferred his typewriter to dinner parties, treating advertising as the most fascinating game he knew—a shift in perspective that turned relentless effort into a source of lifelong satisfaction.
