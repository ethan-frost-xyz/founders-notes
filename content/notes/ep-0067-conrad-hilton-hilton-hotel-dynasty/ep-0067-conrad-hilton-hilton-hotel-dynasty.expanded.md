### 36:04 — Conrad Hilton’s belief that no bad situation is permanent

Context: During the Great Depression, Conrad Hilton was on the brink of losing his entire hotel empire, desperately trying to keep the El Paso Hilton afloat. This remark captures the unwavering optimism that kept him going.

Quote: “He believed that if he could at least keep the El Paso Hilton in business, he might actually be able to ride out the Depression. He was the kind of man who always believed that **no bad situation to be permanent**. Again, another trait, I think, that we should all steal.” (36:04)

Key takeaway: Hilton’s conviction that hardship is temporary was a survival mechanism that allowed him to persist through financial devastation. For founders, adopting a mindset of impermanence can provide the resilience needed to weather crises and avoid despair.

### 46:20 — Conrad Hilton’s deep regret for not spending time with family or finding a spouse

Context: Alone on New Year’s Eve 1941 in a New York hotel bar, Hilton watched happy couples and felt the weight of his solitary life, realizing that his relentless pursuit of business had cost him personal fulfillment.

Quote: “While he had previously done a lot with his life, all around him, it was glaringly obvious that others had somehow done what he hadn’t. They had forged genuine relationships with spouses and partners with whom they were now happily sharing their lives. Maybe none of them had his money, but they seemed to have much more. When he put his life under heavy scrutiny, he didn’t like what he saw. **If only he had been at home with his sons, perhaps he would have felt better about these things.**” (46:20)

Key takeaway: Even monumental professional success cannot substitute for deep personal relationships. Hilton’s regret serves as a stark reminder to founders that career ambition must be balanced with intentional investment in family and intimate connections to avoid a hollow legacy.

### 57:11 — Trusting your gut: the little voice in our heads holds the answers

Context: Throughout his life, Hilton attributed his business acumen to his spiritual practice of prayer and listening. He explained how he made major decisions only after quiet deliberation, equating the inner voice to answered prayers.

Quote: “‘It’s not enough to just pray,’ he once observed. ‘You have to be able to listen. Key takeaway: Hilton’s method highlights that intuition is not a mystical gift but a discipline—cultivating the ability to quiet external noise and hear one’s inner guidance can lead to more aligned and effective decisions.
