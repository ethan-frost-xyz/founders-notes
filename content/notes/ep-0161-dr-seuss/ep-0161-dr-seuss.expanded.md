### 38:00 — Frank Capra taught Ted Geisel tight, concise storytelling

Context: During World War II, Geisel worked under acclaimed director Frank Capra making propaganda films. Capra’s editing philosophy—underlining only what advanced the story—was a revelation to Geisel and permanently shaped his approach to writing, where every word had to earn its place.

Quote: "He would bring Capra his first draft of a script for a film. Then, watch with near reverence as Capra slowly went through it with a pencil. 'The first thing you have to do in writing is find out if you're saying anything,' he told Ted and Capra would carefully go through Ted's script underlining the places where he advanced the story. The rest he left unlined. Most of the first draft would be returned to him with little or no underlining. '**But Capra taught me conciseness,**' said Ted." (38:00)

Key takeaway: Capra’s brutal editing method—highlighting only story-advancing material—instilled in Geisel a powerful discipline that later defined his children’s books: writing 1,000 pages to get 60 that worked. The lesson for any creator is to rigorously strip away anything that doesn’t move the narrative or idea forward. Conciseness isn’t just shorter; it’s respecting your audience’s time and intelligence, a principle that made Dr. Seuss’s seemingly simple books unforgettable.
