## Expanded datapoints

### 30:15 — Best time to expand is when nobody else dared to take risk

Context: During the panic of 1873, a severe economic depression, Carnegie was building his first steel mill. The downturn reduced demand for materials and labor, lowering his construction costs by 25%. This experience taught him a counter-cyclical investment lesson.

Quote: "For Carnegie, it was another invaluable business lesson. **The best time to expand was when no one else dared to take the risks.** And he was able to do this because he was a -- he had legendary levels for frugality." (30:15)

Key takeaway: Carnegie’s insight is that moments of widespread fear and retreat by competitors create the best opportunities for expansion, as costs are lower and less competition exists. This principle applies broadly: acting decisively when others are paralyzed can yield durable advantages.

### 35:15 — Revenue and profits are cyclical but costs are forever

Context: Carnegie’s management philosophy centered on fanatical cost accounting. He believed that while market prices and profits fluctuate, cost savings are permanent and under direct control. This mantra was repeated throughout his career.

Quote: "Carnegie would repeat the mantra time and time again. **Profits and prices were cyclical**, subject to any number of transient forces of the marketplace. Costs, however, could be strictly controlled. And in Carnegie's view, any savings achieved in the cost of goods were permanent." (35:15)

Key takeaway: By prioritizing cost control over revenue or profit, a business builds a resilient foundation that endures through market cycles. Permanent savings compound over time, providing a structural advantage that transient revenue spikes cannot match.

### 1:07:45 — Assassination attempt on Frick

Context: After the violent Homestead strike, a Russian anarchist shot and stabbed Henry Clay Frick in his office. Despite serious wounds, Frick refused immediate medical care, finished his work, and issued a statement to reporters before being taken home.

Quote: "And even then, he had his litter bearers stop so that he might issue a statement to the reporters. **'This incident will not change the attitude of Carnegie Steel Company towards the Amalgamated Association.'** 'I do not think I shall die, but whether I do or not, the company will pursue the same policy and it will win.'" (1:07:45)

Key takeaway: Frick’s response demonstrates extreme resolve and an unwavering commitment to his position, but it also reveals the dangers of intransigence when conflict escalates. While such determination can be effective in business, the episode illustrates how personal and labor disputes can spiral into tragic, avoidable violence.
