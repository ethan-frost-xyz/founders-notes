## Expanded datapoints

### 31:00 — Almost missed the Voisey's Bay discovery because Friedland was distracted

Context: In the early 1990s, Robert Friedland's company Diamond Fields was funding multiple prospecting ventures. While prospectors in Labrador were trying to alert him to a potentially massive find, Friedland was distracted by other projects, including a diamond operation. The prospectors had to repeatedly call the office to get attention for what would become the largest nickel deposit in North American history.

Quote: "To the prospectors' dismay, Diamond Fields continued to dither." (31:00)

Key takeaway: Even when you have a process for generating opportunities, you can miss the biggest one if you are not paying close attention. Friedland nearly fumbled a discovery worth over $4 billion because his focus was scattered across too many ventures. The lesson for operators is that maintaining focus and responsiveness to early signals from your team is critical, as the highest-upside outcome may not be the one currently consuming your attention.

### 37:00 — Reputation changes with success: from penny-stock promoter to serial success

Context: After the Voisey's Bay discovery and sale, Robert Friedland's public image shifted dramatically. Earlier in his career, he was viewed as a penny-stock promoter with a trail of failed ventures and environmental disasters. Post-success, he was described as a serial success, philanthropist, and foundation leader, illustrating how financial outcomes can rewrite a person's reputation.

Quote: "Robert Friedland, the Canadian mine promoter, is known for pushing ventures that later stumble." (37:00)

Key takeaway: Success has a powerful retroactive effect on how a person is perceived. Friedland went from being a pariah associated with shareholder losses and environmental damage to a respected figure once he generated a multi-billion-dollar outcome. This highlights that in business, a single enormous win can overwrite years of negative history, and reputation is often more malleable than it appears.

### 49:00 — Control your costs for a massive competitive advantage

Context: During the bidding war for Voisey's Bay, Inco analyzed what would happen if their rival Falconbridge acquired the deposit. The internal projections showed that Falconbridge's cost per pound of nickel would drop dramatically, giving them a structural advantage that could threaten Inco's entire business. This realization forced Inco to raise their bid to $4.5 billion to secure the asset.

Quote: "For the first time in its existence, Falconbridge would be producing nickel at substantially lower costs than Inco, $0.55 per pound less. Falconbridge would be significantly more profitable and in a dangerously strong position to cut into Inco's market share and profits by slashing nickel prices." (49:00)

Key takeaway: A lower cost structure is one of the most durable competitive advantages a business can have. Inco was willing to pay a premium of billions because losing the low-cost deposit to a competitor would have allowed Falconbridge to undercut them on price and steal market share. This echoes lessons from industrialists like Carnegie and Rockefeller: if you can produce profitably at a price your competitors cannot match, you control the market.
