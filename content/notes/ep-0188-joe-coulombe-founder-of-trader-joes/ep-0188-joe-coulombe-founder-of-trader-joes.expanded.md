### 7:00 — Milk companies blackmailed customers to retain business

Context: In 1960s California, the milk industry was deeply corrupt, and Joe Coulombe’s supplier Merritt Adamson Jr. was a rare ethical player. The shocking blackmail tactic used to keep grocery stores locked into milk contracts illustrates the environment in which Coulombe’s early chain, Pronto Markets, was operating.

Quote: "They'd hire prostitutes, give them a hotel room, film them having sex with the prostitutes. Then they would keep that footage secret so if they ever -- if their customers ever went and said, 'Hey, we're switching business to this other milk company,' they'd be like, 'Oh, yes, no, you're not because here, I have this video of you with this prostitute.' **So they're blackmailing their own customers just so they could sell them more milk.** I mean it's just absolutely crazy." (7:00)

Key takeaway: Blackmail replaced fair competition in the milk trade, revealing how established industries can harbor extraordinary corruption. For Coulombe, understanding this hidden, unethical dynamic underscored the value of relationships built on trust, and it reinforced his later commitment to high-value, differentiated products that didn’t depend on such coercive supplier relationships.

### 22:00 — Adopt a reasonable strategy instead of waiting for an optimal one and stick with it

Context: After reading Barbara Tuchman’s *The Guns of August*, Coulombe extracted a timeless management insight. He applied it to his own business crisis, deciding that waiting for the perfect move while 7-Eleven invaded his market would be fatal. Instead, he chose a workable path and committed to it.

Quote: "The most basic conclusion I drew from her book was that **if you adopt a reasonable strategy as opposed to waiting for an optimum strategy and stick with it, you'll probably succeed.** Tenacity is as important as brilliance." (22:00)

Key takeaway: Pursuing an optimal, “classical” solution often leads to paralysis in a fast-changing environment. Coulombe’s takeaway—that a reasonable strategy executed with tenacity beats waiting for perfect conditions—became a foundational entrepreneurial principle. It gave him permission to act decisively and shape Trader Joe’s even when uncertainty remained.

### 24:00 — Seeking the optimal solution is a waste of time; his core value was high wages

Context: Coulombe realized that Pronto Markets needed a guiding star, not a flawless master plan. Borrowing from the non‑convex problems concept in *The Guns of August*, he rejected endless optimization and chose a single, powerful commitment: paying employees well.

Quote: "I concluded that I didn't have to find an optimal solution to Pronto's difficulties, just a reasonable one. **Trying to find an optimal solution in business is a waste of time.** The factors in the equation are changing all the time. But you've got to have something to hang your hat on. The one core value that I chose was our high compensation policies." (24:00)

Key takeaway: The search for optimality distracts from the more practical work of building on a solid, differentiating principle. Coulombe’s high‑wage policy became the core value that reduced turnover, attracted better people, and distinguished Trader Joe’s. The lesson is that anchoring a business to one meaningful, non‑obvious standard can be more powerful than any spreadsheet‑perfect plan.

### 40:00 — Worry not about your enemy, but your own troops knowing your plan

Context: Drawing on philosopher José Ortega y Gasset and General Patton, Coulombe stresses that internal alignment matters more than external secrecy. Throughout his career, he saw his role as selling a “plan of action and program of collaboration” to everyone—from bankers to store employees.

Quote: "From the beginning, thanks to Ortega, I've been aware of the need to sell everybody. I took a cue from General Patton who thought that **the greatest danger was not that the enemy would learn his plans but that his own troops would not.** Most of my career has been spent selling plans of action and programs of collaboration, whether to Rexall to start up Pronto Markets or Bank of America to buy out Pronto or landlords or vendors, many of whom were skeptical, if not hostile, to my plans, and above all to my employees." (40:00)

Key takeaway: A strategy is worthless if the team doesn’t understand or believe in it. Coulombe’s Patton‑inspired insight shifted his focus from guarding secrets to relentless internal communication. The bigger risk is not competitive espionage but disengaged or uninformed employees who can’t execute the vision. This philosophy underpinned Trader Joe’s cult‑like consistency across stores, even as the chain grew.
