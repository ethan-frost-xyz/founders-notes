### 7:00 — Microsoft missed the internet

Context: As Bill Gates approaches 40, his company is under antitrust scrutiny and he is about to make one of the biggest strategic misjudgments of his career—completely overlooking the commercial internet despite his legendary ability to study industry shifts.

Quote: "The first thing is that he **completely missed the Internet,** which is surprising given how pivotal the Internet was or is and how smart Bill Gates is, right?" (7:00)

Key takeaway: Even the most voracious students of business history can miss a paradigm shift when they become too anchored to their own assumptions. Gates’s lapse shows that intelligence and information access aren’t enough—you must actively question whether your current focus is still the right one.

### 9:00 — Products don’t compete, companies do

Context: Bill Joy, co-founder of Sun Microsystems, is criticizing Microsoft’s software quality, but the host reminds listeners that battles are won by corporate machinery—distribution, resources, and sheer determination—more than by the elegance of a single product.

Quote: "Even if you think their product is not as good, they -- usually history tells us that those companies are more likely to win than not, right? **Really, the main point of me bringing this to your attention is because we have to remember that products don’t compete and that companies do.** So as Joy compared Microsoft software to the cars built by General Motors before the Japanese entered the market." (9:00)

Key takeaway: A startup’s superior product is often insufficient to unseat an incumbent with massive distribution and resources. The lesson is that competition is a company-level game—culture, economics, and sales infrastructure matter as much as the code or the design.

### 10:00 — Joy’s law of innovation

Context: Bill Joy articulates a structural vulnerability of large firms: as headcount grows, the density of true A-players inevitably declines, a principle later echoed by Steve Jobs (“bozo explosion”) and Elon Musk (who personally interviewed the first 3,000 SpaceX employees).

Quote: "So it's this thing termed Joy's law of innovation, right? This is very fascinating. **Joy's law of innovation was that the number of bright people in any company went down as the size went up.** So we've seen this over and over again." (10:00)

Key takeaway: Scaling a company unavoidably dilutes the average talent level, which is why startups can often out-innovate giants. The best founders fight this trend by obsessing over hiring standards, knowing that the per-capita creativity that wins early won’t survive bureaucratic growth without deliberate effort.

It's going to be published, I think, in 1997, right, maybe 1998. **It never mentions the Internet.**" (38:00)

Key takeaway: Even the smartest founders can commit publicly to a wrong thesis, making it harder to pivot later. Writing a book that omitted the internet is a stark reminder that intellectual investment creates inertia—when you’ve spent years championing one future, you’re naturally slow to see its replacement.

### 38:00 — Gates was thinking about Starlink at that same time

Context: While missing the terrestrial internet, Gates was simultaneously funding a satellite-based “celestial information highway” with Craig McCaw, aiming to blanket the globe with connectivity decades before Elon Musk’s Starlink.

Quote: "It gets to the idea let's give everybody Internet access all over the globe 24/7 through satellites that are in low earth orbit. Bill Gates tried to do the same thing in the 1990s. **Gates and McCaw had been working on a celestial information highway that would bring video, voice, and data transmissions to millions of people in the most remote parts of the world using satellites in the low earth orbit.**" (38:00)

Key takeaway: The core idea behind Starlink wasn’t new—it was already in Gates’s mind. This illustrates that visionary concepts can arrive too early, before the technology or market is ready, and that timing is often as critical as the idea itself. Gates’s satellite venture serves as a caution that being right too soon can be indistinguishable from being wrong.
