## Expanded datapoints

### 22:03 — The idea of “standard” is a construct

Context: Evan Spiegel is recounting a costly mistake from Snapchat’s early funding, when Lightspeed’s Jeremy Liew included terms that gave the firm control over the next round. Evan accepted the terms after being told they were “standard,” a lesson he later shared with students.

Quote: “When I asked a question because I didn’t understand something, I was reassured that the term was standard and, therefore, agreeable. I forgot that **the idea of standard is a construct.** It simply does not exist.” (22:03)

Key takeaway: The notion of “standard” is often used to deflect scrutiny and push through unfavorable terms. Entrepreneurs must interrogate every clause themselves rather than relying on reassurances, because what is presented as industry norm may simply be a negotiating tactic that can trap the unprepared.

### 1:00:05 — Privacy is the understanding of context

Context: In a 2014 keynote, Evan Spiegel draws on the work of privacy scholar Helen Nissenbaum to distinguish privacy from secrecy, arguing that real privacy is about respecting the boundaries of different relationships.

Quote: “Unfortunately, privacy is too often articulated as secrecy. When as Nissenbaum points out, **privacy is actually focused on an understanding of context,** not what is said, but where it is said and to whom.” (1:00:05)

Key takeaway: Privacy isn’t about hiding information; it’s about sharing appropriately within specific contexts, just as people naturally do in face-to-face life. Digital platforms that collapse all contexts into one public feed violate this fundamental human need, and designing for contextual integrity can create more authentic communication.

### 1:02:29 — Social media is the result of capitalism getting into our relationships

Context: Concluding his keynote, Evan critiques how social media platforms turn personal expression into a popularity contest, forcing people to curate a consistent brand rather than embrace their natural contradictions.

Quote: “Popular expression becomes the most valuable expression. **Social media businesses represent an aggressive expansion of capitalism into our personal relationships.** We are asked to perform for our friends, to create things they like, to work on a personal brand.” (1:02:29)

Key takeaway: When social media quantifies feelings with likes and shares, it commodifies human connection, pressuring people to perform a marketable self. This insight explains why impermanent, like-free spaces feel liberating: they reject the idea that relationships should be optimized for engagement, allowing the messy, changing reality of being human.
