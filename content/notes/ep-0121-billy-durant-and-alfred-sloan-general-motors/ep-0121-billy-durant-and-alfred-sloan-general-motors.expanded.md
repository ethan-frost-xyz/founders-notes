### 1:17:00 — Sloan studied dealer feedback personally to ground his decisions

Context: As head of GM, Sloan made a relentless practice of visiting dealers across the country, listening to their feedback, and then meticulously reviewing his notes. This first-hand intelligence loop kept him connected to the market reality.

Quote: "I would meet them in their own place of business, talk with them across their own desks and ask them for suggestions and criticisms concerning their relations with the corporation, the character of the product, the corporation's policies, the trends of consumer demand, their view of the future and many other things in the interest of the business. I made careful notes of all the points that came up. And when I got back home, **I studied them.**" (1:17:00)

Key takeaway: Direct, unfiltered input from the front lines, followed by disciplined personal study, gives leaders an edge that reports and second-hand summaries cannot replicate. Sloan’s routine turned dealer conversations into a proprietary intelligence system that fed better product and policy decisions.

### 1:18:00 — Sloan on change: no fixed rule replaces sound judgment

Context: Sloan reflected on the perpetual nature of change and the danger of treating organizational structure as a static formula. He insisted that management is a dynamic, case-by-case discipline, not an exercise in applying fixed rules.

Quote: "**No company ever stopped changing. Change will come for better or worse.** I hope I have not left the impression that the organization runs itself automatically. An organization does not make decisions, its function is to provide a framework based upon established criteria within which decisions can be fashioned in an orderly manner. The task of management is not to apply a formula but to decide an issue on a case-by-case basis. No fixed inflexible rule can ever be substituted for the exercise of sound business judgment in the decision-making process." (1:18:00)

Key takeaway: Change is inevitable, and the only sustainable response is a culture of judgment, not a rulebook. Sloan’s philosophy warns against bureaucratic rigidity; leaders must treat each decision as a fresh exercise of context-aware intelligence, always adapting the framework to the moment.
