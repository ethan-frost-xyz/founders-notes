### 59:22 — The ice entrepreneur of New England and the vision to ship ice to India

Context: Paul English’s home by Spy Pond once belonged to Addison Gage, a 19th‑century ice entrepreneur who harvested and exported ice globally. Paul uses this history to illustrate that even seemingly absurd ideas can succeed with a large market and the right technology.

Quote: "Paul knew some of the history and would entertain guests with it, drawing a moral, no matter how wacky -- no matter how wacky an idea can succeed if it aims at a big market, all those hot places well of refrigeration and if there are technologies to execute it, special saws, sawdust in the hold of sailing ships insulated buildings. **What vision the ice bearings of New England must have had to look at the ice on Spy Pond and think, I could send this to India.**" (59:22)

Key takeaway: The ice trade demonstrates that entrepreneurial vision often looks ridiculous at first, but when paired with a massive addressable market and enabling technology, it can create entirely new industries. Paul’s retelling of this story underscores his belief that founders should not dismiss ideas simply because they seem outlandish—market size and execution matter more than conventional plausibility.
