### 13:00 — Leonardo da Vinci on experience as the true teacher over book learning

Context: Early in the monologue, Leonardo’s lack of formal schooling is introduced. He calls himself an “unlettered man” with irony and takes pride in being a “disciple of experience.” This moment captures his contempt for those who rely solely on received wisdom, a theme that runs through his notebooks.

Quote: "“I’m fully aware that my not being a man of letters may cause certain presumptuous people to think that they may with reason blame me alleging that I am a man without learning. Foolish folk, they strut about puffed up and pompous, decked out and adorned not with their own labors, but by those of others. ### 25:07 — Leonardo’s autopsy of a centenarian yields the first known description of arteriosclerosis

Context: In 1508, Leonardo began a second round of anatomical dissections. After chatting with a man over 100 who died peacefully, he dissected the body and documented the hardened arteries and withered organs. This passage shows how his direct, experience-based method led to a landmark medical discovery.

Quote: "“I made an autopsy in order to ascertain the cause of so peaceful a death and found it that it proceeded from weakness throughout the failure of blood and of the artery that feeds the heart and the other lower members, which I found to be very dry, shrunken, and withered,” he wrote. “Next to a drawing of the veins in the right arm, he compared the centenarian's blood vessels to those of a 2-year-old boy who had also died at a hospital. He found those of the boy to be supple and unconstricted, contrary to what I found in the old man.” **This constriction of blood flow had caused, among other things, the centenarian's liver to become so dry that when it is subjected to even the slightest friction, its substance falls away in tiny flakes like sawdust and leaves behind the veins and the arteries.** The noted medical historian and cardiologist Kenneth Keele called Leonardo's analysis the first description of arteriosclerosis as a function of time." (25:07)

Key takeaway: Leonardo’s hands-on investigation, done without formal medical training, anticipated modern pathology by centuries. By comparing a young boy’s supple vessels with the centenarian’s dried, plaque-like arteries, he linked anatomical changes to aging in a way no one had previously recorded. The broader lesson: disciplined observation and a willingness to look closely at what others overlook can produce breakthroughs that outlast the tools and frameworks of any single era.
