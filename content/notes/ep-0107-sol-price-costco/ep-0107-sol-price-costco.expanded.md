## Expanded datapoints

### 04:30 — Founded and took three companies public

Context: Sol Price, an immigrant’s son who became an attorney, eventually left law to start FedMart, Price Club, and a real‑estate investment trust, each taken public. His career exemplifies an unusual ability to create and scale distinct ventures.

Quote: "He spent the first half of his career as an attorney, which I’ll talk a little bit about today. But also, **he has the distinction that he’s probably one of the few people in the world that founded and took three separate companies public.** So where did he get this drive?" (04:30)

Key takeaway: Taking one company public is rare; doing it three times across different eras signals a founder who repeatedly identified market whitespace and built organizations that could scale. It also underscores a restless, creative energy that kept him starting anew rather than simply managing past successes.

### 12:00 — Learned that he thrived when he was productive

Context: During World War II, Sol worked his law practice in the morning and a defense plant at night. Though grueling, this period taught him that busy, productive days gave him energy and satisfaction.

Quote: "Unlike his father, who had managed to find ways not to work, **Sol thrived when he was busy, working hard and feeling productive.** His desire to work hard and get the most out of every hour of the day was not characteristic of his earlier years." (12:00)

Key takeaway: Productivity can become a source of identity and joy, not just a means to an end. Sol’s realization—that he functioned best when fully occupied—shows that personal work rhythms are often discovered through intense immersion, not idle planning.

### 13:30 — Do it now, there is always enough time

Context: Sol’s granddaughter described how he constantly coached her on time management, and he famously kept a “Do it now” sign on his desk. His philosophy was that we squander far more time than we realize.

Quote: "‘Well, you have enough time,’ he would say. ‘There are 24 hours in a day, 168 hours in a week. We just waste so much of it. **There was always enough time.**’ And so on his desk, he had—he was famous for having this sign that says, do it now." (13:30)

Key takeaway: The feeling of being too busy often masks wasted hours. Sol’s lesson is that clarifying priorities and removing distractions reveals abundant time for what matters. Impatience, channeled into immediate action, becomes a competitive advantage.

### 22:00 — Origin of membership discount shopping

Context: Before starting FedMart, Sol traveled to Los Angeles to study Fedco, a non‑profit membership store for federal employees. The trip crystallized the model he would later adopt and refine.

Quote: "They decided to take a trip to see Fedco. It was a large store located in a nontraditional retail area of Los Angeles near a cow pasture. Fedco was not a for‑profit company. It was a nonprofit corporation operating a membership retail store that catered to federal employees such as postal workers. The Fedco store was doing a brisk business with customers coming as far away as San Diego. In fact, 5,000 Fedco members lived in San Diego, and were driving 200 miles round‑trip to take advantage of Fedco’s bargain pricing. On the way back, the men had a spirited discussion about Fedco, the membership concept, the nonprofit nature of the business and categories of products being sold. **They were convinced that Fedco‑type businesses could be successful in San Diego.**" (22:00)

Key takeaway: The membership discount model wasn’t invented in a vacuum—it was observed, copied, and then improved. Sol’s genius was recognizing that a closed‑loop membership could create loyalty and lower marketing costs while passing savings to customers. Good ideas are often “borrowed” and applied in a new geography.

### 26:00 — Capping downside

Context: When launching FedMart, Sol used his legal experience to negotiate a lease that contained a one‑year escape clause, limiting his personal risk while leaving the upside open.

Quote: "He goes in, and he uses the experiences he had as a lawyer to negotiate leases and to change certain clauses in a lease, so his downside is capped. **‘We negotiated a lease for 10 years. We were so uncertain as to the future of this enterprise that we reserved the right to cancel the lease at the end of one year if it didn’t work.’** So one of the most famous examples of entrepreneurs doing that is, Richard Branson goes to start, I think, it was Virgin Atlantic, whichever one was his first airline, and he negotiates with Boeing." (26:00)

Key takeaway: Structuring early commitments with built‑in exit ramps is a time‑tested entrepreneurial tactic. By capping the worst‑case scenario, a founder can take bold leaps without risking total ruin, preserving the ability to try again or pivot.

### 39:00 — Spend 90% of your time teaching

Context: Jim Sinegal, who worked for Sol from age 18 and later co‑founded Costco, credited Sol with embedding a teaching culture. The chapter on Sol’s leadership opens with this maxim.

Quote: "It starts with a quote from Jim Sinegal. He says, **‘If you’re not spending 90% of your time teaching, you’re not doing your job.’** And so now the author writes, ‘Those of us who were Sol’s students would all agree that Sol, above all else, was a great teacher.’" (39:00)

Key takeaway: Sol viewed leadership as an educational function, not a command‑and‑control role. When a founder invests heavily in explaining why behind the how, employees become better decision‑makers, the organization scales more reliably, and the culture self‑replicates.

### 39:30 — Train an animal, teach a person

Context: Sol drew a sharp distinction between rote training and genuine education. He wanted employees to understand the reasoning behind their work, not just follow orders.

Quote: "Sol had a favorite adage that he frequently found appropriate to repeat. **‘You train an animal, you teach a person.’** Sol really wanted all FedMart employees to think about and understand why their jobs were important to the success of FedMart." (39:30)

Key takeaway: Sustainable excellence comes from comprehension, not blind compliance. Teaching people the purpose of their tasks turns routine roles into meaningful contributions and reduces the need for constant supervision.

### 39:45 — Manuals were a substitute for thinking

Context: Sol distrusted thick procedure manuals, arguing they encouraged people to stop using judgment. His alternative was the “alter ego” concept—teaching employees to think like the owner.

Quote: "He was not a big fan of procedures and training manuals because he believed that **manuals were a substitute for thinking.** His emphasis on teaching was expressed in the phrase, alter ego." (39:45)

Key takeaway: Over‑documented processes can calcify a business and stifle initiative. Instead of scripting every action, a company should cultivate a shared mindset so that front‑line workers can use good judgment when the manual inevitably fails to cover a novel situation.

### 42:30 — Brilliance of fewer items, oil example, cost of managing, etc.

Context: Sol’s “intelligent loss of sales” theory argued that carrying fewer items (e.g., only the large, best‑value size of three‑in‑one oil) increased labor productivity enough to offer lower prices, even if it meant losing the rare customer who insisted on a small size.

Quote: "Sol’s classic example at the time was three in one oil. The manufacturer produced the oil in three sizes. Most stores carried all three sizes of three in one oil, even though the large eight‑ounce size was a better value per ounce than the smaller sizes. Most people who need three in one oil will buy the eight‑ounce size if that’s all there is on the shelf. He said, ‘Most, but not all.’ Remember that point. ‘The price is far better per ounce. Though eight ounces is a lot more for some customers is acceptable for most customers. What about the customer who doesn’t buy the eight‑ounce size? That was the intelligent loss of sales. What does limited selection have to do with efficiency?’ This part is going to remind, if you listen to my podcast I did on Herb Kelleher and all the efficiencies he got from Southwest Airlines and why it was one of the only airlines to pull—to be able to be profitable. He uses a lot of ideas very similar to this one. So I’m going to read this whole paragraph to you, but just keep that in mind as I’m reading to you. ‘What does limited selection have to do with efficiency? Because payroll and benefits represent approximately 80% of retailer’s cost of operations, pricing advantage follows labor productivity. Fewer items result in reduced labor hours throughout the—all of the product supply channels, ordering from suppliers, receiving them at the distribution center, stocking them at the store and checking out the merchandise. **Put simply, the cost to deal with 4,500 items is a lot less than the cost to deal with 50,000 items. That’s the intelligent loss of sales.**’" (42:30)

Key takeaway: Simplification is a multiplier. By deliberately narrowing choices, a retailer can streamline every step of the supply chain and pass dramatic savings to customers. The lesson extends beyond retail: refusing to satisfy every fringe demand often unlocks far greater value for the vast majority of users.

### 44:00 — Sam Walton on Sol Price

Context: Sam Walton explicitly credited Sol as the source of many Walmart innovations, openly admitting he borrowed ideas from FedMart.

Quote: "Let me just read this quote from Sam. He says, **‘I learned a lot from Sol Price, a great operator who had started FedMart in Southern California in 1954. I’ve stolen, I actually prefer the word, borrowed, as many ideas from Sol Price as from anybody else in the business.’** I really liked Sol’s FedMart name, so I latched right onto it with Walmart." (44:00)

Key takeaway: Even the most iconic founders acknowledge their intellectual debts. Walton’s candor shows that originality isn’t about inventing from scratch—it’s about recognizing a superior model and executing it better than anyone else. Great entrepreneurs are voracious learners who borrow and adapt.

### 50:00 — He kinda fumbled the bag after selling

Context: Sol sold FedMart to German retailer Hugo Mann, hoping to fund expansion, but Mann’s controlling style soon led to a humiliating boardroom tirade and Sol’s eventual firing. Selling meant losing the compounding he had built for two decades.

Quote: "Later on, looking back on our times with Mann, the warning signs seemed so obvious, but at the time we were caught up in the euphoria of making a big deal. **The first time that Sol and I experienced a real Hugo Mann was at this Board meeting. Rather than the friendly person we had seen, Mann launched into a 90‑minute tirade criticizing Sol and FedMart’s performance.** He only looked at me, never once looking at Sol or addressing a single remark directly to him." (50:00)

Key takeaway: Selling to a bad cultural fit can destroy the very engine that made the company successful. Sol’s misstep is a cautionary tale about valuing vision alignment over deal size. Founders who maintain control often allow their innovations to compound far longer, while early exits can cede the future to someone who doesn’t understand what they bought.

### 1:05:00 — Impact of other lives, all of us can say that he had a transformative impact

Context: In the book’s closing, Sol’s son Robert reflects that Sol’s most enduring legacy wasn’t the public companies but the life skills and values he imparted to family, employees, and protégés like Jim Sinegal.

Whatever I have learned about business, I learned from my father. **He taught me how to think and how to question and not to fall into the trap of assuming rather than checking things out for myself.** He also taught me to be humble, to appreciate the unpredictability of life, to care for people, to remain hopeful, and always to be there for people who are in need." (1:05:00)

Key takeaway: A founder’s deepest influence often shows up in the people they shaped, not in the institutions they built. Teaching curiosity, skepticism of easy assumptions, and a responsibility to others creates a multiplier effect that outlasts any single enterprise. A life measured by such impact is unmistakably well‑lived.
