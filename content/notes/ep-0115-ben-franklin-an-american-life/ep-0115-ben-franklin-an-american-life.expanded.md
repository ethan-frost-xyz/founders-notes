### 09:00 — Actions should be judged by their contribution to the common good

Context: This is part of the description of Benjamin Franklin’s core personality and moral framework, highlighting his pragmatic and community-oriented values.

Quote: "He liked witty discussion. He hated disorder and mob behavior. **He had a fundamental moral belief that action should be judged by how much they benefit the common good.** Policies that encourage hard work were good, but not because they led to great accumulations of private wealth, they were good because they increased the total well-being of a community and the dignity of every aspiring individual." (09:00)

Key takeaway: Franklin measured the worth of any action by its contribution to the collective good, not merely private gain. This principle guided his entrepreneurial and civic efforts, reminding us that lasting success is often tied to the value we create for others.

### 34:00 — Ben Franklin’s pro/con method for difficult decisions

Context: Franklin described a systematic approach to decision-making that he used throughout his life, treating choices like a bookkeeper’s ledger to clarify the best path.

Quote: "I love this idea. I heard about this idea years before I even read a book about Ben Franklin. It's called -- it's the Ben Franklin Method for Making Difficult Decisions, and he's describing this method to us. **'My way is to divide a paper into 2 columns, writing over one column pro and the other column con.** Then he would list all the arguments on each side and weigh how important each was. … Where I find 2, one on each side that seems equal, I strike them both out. Key takeaway: A structured, almost mathematical approach to weighing pros and cons can remove emotional bias and bring clarity to complex choices. Franklin’s method is a timeless tool for making rational, well-considered decisions.
