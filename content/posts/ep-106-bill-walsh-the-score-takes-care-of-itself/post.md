Founders #106 Bill Walsh - NFL

“Champions behave like champions before they’re champions. They have a winning standard of performance before they are winners.”

Your org must have a clear structure and philosophy before focusing on results. “The score will take care of itself.”

If you love sports, this episode is a must-listen. If not, you should still know Bill Walsh's principles.

More on standards:

The principles of a high-performance organization are universal. (16:15)

Like water, many decent individuals will seek lower ground if left to their own inclinations. Humans require high standards to succeed. (36:00)

The Success Disease. After winning multiple Super Bowls, the media labeled Walsh a genius. He identified this praise as an “albatross around my neck” that could lead to arrogance and distract him from the hard work that got him there. 
You must maintain your own internal scorecard (Buffett) and refuse to let external praise or criticism define your reality. (50:15)

Extreme success often comes with an emotional toll. Knowing when to walk away often decides whether a Founder’s story becomes a cautionary tale of sacrifice. Walsh retired at the peak of his career, right after winning his third Super Bowl, because the internal pressure of maintaining his “Standard of Performance” had become too high to sustain. He chose to walk away to protect himself instead of letting the stress ruin him, turning down blank check offers from across the NFL. (1:04:00)

The score will take care of itself. “A pretty package can’t sell a poor product.” Walsh realized winning was the only way he could convince fans to buy tickets. (1:03:00)

Other notes on Bill Walsh:

You have an obligation to teach everything you know to those around you. (14:00)

“A game changer takes a while to change the way the game is played.” Initially Walsh faced a lot of criticism for his West Coast offense. Walsh pressed on. 40 years later, his influence can still be seen on the field. (44:00)

Don’t waste time thinking about your competitors. (48:30)

There is no true mastery, it’s a process. Never stop improving. (53:30)

“In evaluating people, I prize ego. It often translates into a fierce desire to do their best and an inner confidence that stands them in good stead when things really get rough.” (55:00)
