Founders #108: Jim Simons - Renaissance Technologies

Even world-class intellects aren’t immune to impostor syndrome. Simons nearly quit a decade into his second career before launching the greatest fund of all time, which delivered a 66% average annual return from 1988 to 2018.

Simons nearly gave up before he became the greatest fund manager ever.
At around 42 years old, Jim Simons had already left a prestigious, tenured career in mathematics to pursue trading full-time.

After a decade of trading, his new algorithmic system fails disastrously, causing his fund to hemorrhage money.

Facing angry clients and deep personal humiliation, he and his team lose faith in their thesis, leading Simons to explicitly question whether he should abandon the entire idea of automated trading.

The path to outlier success is through the valley of death where you temporary logic and emotion suggest quitting. (33:00-39:00)

How to get back on track.
He doubled down on his algorithmic trading, relying on his core competency in mathematics, and launched his flagship Medallion Fund in 1988, which has now earned over $100 billion in trading profits since then.

Two decades prior, Simons’ son died tragically. He learned then that adversity is rarely unique; it’s a universal human experience, so keep pushing through the pain. (11:30)

How to manage feeling overwhelmed.
Simons would lie motionless in a dark room for hours, letting his mind wander through complex problems. I have found that even 15-30 minutes spent like this is a powerful tool. Wear an eye mask and ear plugs. (8:00)

Markets always evolve.
Markets are biological, not mechanical.

Your edge is always eroding because competitors (invasive species) are constantly entering your territory, requiring you to perpetually evolve to survive. (55:00)

Emperors want empires.
You don't get into this business to make a little bit of money. You want to conquer the world. (56:00)

“Be guided by beauty. It can be the way a company runs, or the way an experiment comes out. There's a sense of beauty when something is working well.” (1:03:30)
