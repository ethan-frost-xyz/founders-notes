Founders #143: Alfred Lee Loomis

Before the US recognized the Nazi threat, a former banker invested his fortune to establish a research lab, which led to the development of microwave radar.

Loomis's early foresight and investments completely changed the course of WWII.

Capitalize on inevitabilities.
As worries about the economy increased in the 1920s, Loomis treated the Great Depression as an inevitability rather than a timing exercise. Loomis did not try to predict the exact date of the crash to squeeze out a few extra dollars.

He recognized the inevitable. Had greed gotten the best of Loomis, the radar breakthrough would at least have been delayed. (18:00)

The radar won the war, and the atomic bomb ended it.
Loomis used his money and connections to cut through the patent disputes and red tape that usually stall fast-moving projects. By handling the administrative headaches himself, he allowed his top scientists to concentrate solely on building the radar.

Many who were involved in developing the radar went on to work in the Manhattan Project. (13:00)

Influence the influencers.
To build his secret lab team, Loomis first recruited Nobel laureate Ernest Lawrence, using his prestige to attract elite physicists who might otherwise ignore a financier. Once Lawrence joined, many followed. (36:00)
