Founders #134: Edwin Land - Polaroid

Edwin Land viewed his company as an extension of his scientific soul.

When Polaroid's survival was at risk, Land saw suing Kodak a sacred duty to defend their innovations.

Here are ideas from Land's legendary patent lawsuit:

Defend your foundation without apology.
Kodak tried to crush Polaroid by stealing their instant film patents.

Land fought back by taking the stand and delivering a captivating lesson in optics that left the judge captivated.

Adjusted for inflation, the case would be one of the largest payouts in patent law history ($2.2B).

"Our manifest duty to our shareholders is vigorously to assert our patents." - Land (11:00)

Never underestimate worthy rivals.
Land respected Kodak's prowess despite his advantages, while they fatally dismissed the obsessive creator. (55:00)

Tackle impossible challenges. 
Land built the Polaroid camera around ambitious idea of fulfilling the unarticulated human need to see a photograph instantly. The idea came from his daughter asking to see a picture that was just taken.  (3:00)

Guard against stultification.
Avoid those whose spark has dulled. "They have been stultified somehow and the world is going by them." (15:00)

Begin with the ideal vision, work backwards. 
"You always start with a fantasy. Part of the fantasy technique is to visualize something as perfect. Then with the experiments you work back from that fantasy to reality, hacking away at the components." - Land (21:30)

Frame business as bold experimentation. 
Innovation thrives on embracing failure as a path to success, enduring setbacks with resilience. An essential aspect of creativity is not being afraid to fail. (22:30)

Dismiss mockery from incumbents. 
Revolutionary tools often start as "toys" in the eyes of experts, but they redefine industries. (25:00)

Leverage your advantages beyond obvious markets.
Land's optics expertise birthed the U-2 spy plane, proving founders can reshape history in unexpected arenas. (28:00)
