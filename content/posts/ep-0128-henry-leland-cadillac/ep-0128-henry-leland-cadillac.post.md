Founders #128: Henry Leland (Cadillac / Lincoln)

Henry Leland’s standards were so high that other car tycoons chose to drive his Cadillacs instead of the cars from their own companies.

Here are his three best ideas:

Sweat Blood for Superior Work
“It is easy to get cooperation for mediocre work, but one must sweat blood for the chance to produce a superior product.” 

Leland rejected half his own output when starting his foundry because it was not good enough. (30:00)

Sell By Teaching
Leland never acted like a salesman. 

He visited factories, taught managers how to use machines better, and often improved their entire manufacturing system. 

Orders followed naturally. (20:00)

Master Yourself First
“The one essential ingredient of success was mastery of one’s self as well as one’s job.” 

Technical skill without strong character and self-discipline was worthless to him. (45:00)

Bonus Lesson on Investing an Nascent Industries.
About 2,000 automobile companies emerged in the U.S. between 1894 and 1930, but fierce competition, the Great Depression, and economies of scale narrowed the industry down to just three major players:

1) General Motors, $GM; 2) Ford, $F; and 3) Chrysler, $STLA.

Leland’s focus on precision is why Cadillac-- which helped GM survive tough times-- was able to make it. (33:00)
