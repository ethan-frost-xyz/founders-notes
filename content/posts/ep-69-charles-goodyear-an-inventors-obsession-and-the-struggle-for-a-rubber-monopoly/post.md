Founders #69: Charles Goodyear (creator of vulcanized rubber)

If you are interested in market bubbles, here’s my primer on a sleeper pick beyond the scope of the podcast.

In 1833, America fell into a "Rubber Fever," convinced that this new miracle substance was the universal material for the future. While waterproof shoes were legitimately hyped, the craze quickly spiraled into absurdity: businessmen pitched rubber roofs that would never leak and rubber carriage bodies that would never rot. It wasn't just seen as a new product; optimists viewed it as a fundamental replacement for wood, leather, metal, and more.

Capital flooded into the sector to fund "The Monster," a 30-ton steam-powered machine designed to ramp up production immediately. The market was so intoxicated by the theoretical possibilities that they ignored the lack of basic durability testing. They scaled up factories before the chemistry had actually stabilized. (This was the “Peak of Inflated Expectations” stage of the Gartner Hype Cycle.)

The reality check came during the heatwave of 1834, exposing a catastrophic flaw: untreated rubber melts. The "impervious" rubber roofs turned into black sludge dripping onto residents, and the government-contracted mailbags fused into solid bricks that had to be split apart with axes. The miracle material turned out to be thermally unstable. The collapse was total. Stock prices hit zero, factories closed, and rubber became a synonym for fraud. The entire sector evaporated as quickly as it had appeared.

It took a bankrupt obsessive named Charles Goodyear, experimenting in a jail cell, to finally discover vulcanization years later. The industry eventually recovered and changed the world, but the real breakthrough didn't happen during the speculative boom.

Key note: the Goodyear tire company was founded years later by a different man, Frank Seiberling, who named it after Charles Goodyear. This episode is really a cautionary tale, as Charles failed to capture the value he created.
