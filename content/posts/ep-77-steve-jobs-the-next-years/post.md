He hired a full-time interior designer as one of his first ten employees and spent more time on his high-end office design than on key product decisions. He abandoned the pressure of frugality from the start.

At a major education conference, NeXT had no prototype to display, so Jobs instead handed out an 18-page brochure explaining the company's new logo.

Jobs ignored the failure of the Apple Lisa, repeating the same mistakes. Universities explicitly told Jobs they needed a computer under $3,000, but he dismissed their advice and built a machine costing between $7,000 and $10,000.

Jobs insisted on manufacturing the hardware in-house in San Francisco because he believed customers would travel to the factory to watch their computer being built, like a BMW.

When engineers tried to report realistic delays, Jobs would berate them, causing his team to lie about timelines to avoid his anger.

While the company was struggling to sell computers, Jobs spent 20 minutes arguing with landscapers about sprinkler placement while executives waited for him.

Jobs claimed NeXT had its first profitable quarter (heavily adjusted), fooling the public and his own employees into thinking the business was healthy just before laying off over 60% of the staff.

This was the only correct decision in the episode, as dropping the hardware division was critical, but it was not Jobs’ idea.
