Founders #145: William Randolph Hearst

Hearst realized news could be sold as theater.

He built a media machine that reached millions and shaped what people read, feared, wanted, and argued about.

It helped push the country toward the Spanish-American War.

Three key takeaways you should know:

Invest in top talent.
His father struck a fortune in a gold mine, giving William a safer setup than Pulitzer.

He advocated to his father, George, urging him to change the strategy for the San Francisco Examiner.

William is arguing against his father's frugality in staffing, insisting that hiring top-tier employees is necessary for a successful turnaround.

This talent focus became a staple of his empire, as he could scale his talent's output across media (print, radio, film, etc.) to achieve a unique economy of scope benefit.

Hearst understood that acquiring top human capital, even at a massive premium, was the fastest way to build a competitive moat and transform a failing asset into a market leader. (13:00)

Copy proven ideas from competitors.
Instead of trying to invent a new playbook from scratch, he looks directly to Joseph Pulitzer's successful strategies in New York.

Costco, Walmart, and Amazon all copied each other, deliberately taking the best ideas from one another and from competitors. Also, on a personal level, it reminds us that just because someone is already building something similar doesn’t mean you can’t do it too. (25:00)

He didn't learn from his past, and it cost him everything.
Decades of unchecked spending, massive debt accumulation, and a refusal to cut costs during the Great Depression finally catch up to Hearst in his seventies.

His complete lack of capital discipline forces his creditors to step in and seize control of his entire media empire to avoid total liquidation. (59:00)

Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it,” - George Santayana
