Founders #142: Teddy Roosevelt vs J.P. Morgan

Today, many people confuse a rising market with a healthy society.

Roosevelt believed instead that the economy must serve the citizens first:

"Government stands for manhood first and for business only as an adjunct of manhood."

Morgan viewed the economy first.
In his final years, amid criticism and economic turmoil, Morgan held onto his father's advice about America's endless growth.

"There may be times when things are dark and cloudy in America... remember that the growth of that vast country will take care of it all." (51:00)

The strong rule the weak, but the wise rule the strong.
Morgan reorganized nearly every bankrupt railroad east of the Mississippi, amassing revenues that rivaled half the U.S. government's.

It seemed every poorly run railroad fell under Morgan's control, except James J. Hill.

Despite being a smaller business, Hill was a wise operator and withstood the financial pressure of the recession.

Hill became a co-architect with Morgan in creating the Northern Securities Trust, securing his generational wealth. (16:00)

How to be wise.
Hill's relentless pursuit of knowledge gave him an edge in a highly competitive market.

For years, he would sleep at every stop on his routes each day, staying close to the customer around the clock.

Extra take: These are the types of people you'll compete with in the hottest industries (AI, fintech, healthcare, etc.), and the ones who will survive when the sky is falling.

- From my notes on episode #96 about Hill.

Two stubborn sides cost everyone more.
The 1902 coal strike lasted 164 days, bleeding millions in wages and profits as unions and owners refused to compromise.

Teddy (supporting the miners) and Morgan couldn't reach an agreement, mostly because of their egos, leading to unnecessary value loss for the common people. (42:00)
