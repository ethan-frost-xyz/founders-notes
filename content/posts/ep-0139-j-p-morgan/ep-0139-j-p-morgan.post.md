Founders #139: J.P. Morgan

Morgan's cynicism helped him navigate finance by always seeking hidden motives behind actions.

Morgan understood this because he was his own example.

Before the Federal Reserve existed, the United States relied on Morgan to act as its central bank:

He gained all his power throughout his life by understanding one truth:

Power comes from others' weakness.
He saved while others spent, knowing human nature would eventually cause economic crashes, which he exploited with his preserved war chest of capital.

Morgan reorganized nearly every bankrupt railroad east of the Mississippi during the 1893 recession, amassing revenues rivaling half the U.S. government's. (41:00)

In the Panic of 1907, the semi-retired Morgan bailed out trusts, brokerages, New York City, and the stock exchange, proving that authority stems from being the lender of last resort. (53:00)

At his peak, Morgan controlled 72 directorships across 112 corporations, prompting a congressional investigation into the sheer scale of his monopoly.

People have good, real reasons.
"A man always has two reasons for the things he does: a good one and the real one." (52:00)
