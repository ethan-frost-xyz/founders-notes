Founders #130: Walter Chrysler

In 1901, Chrysler saved just $60 at age 26 while earning 30 cents an hour. Fifteen years later, he was earning $600,000 a year as the highest-paid person in the automobile industry. (23:00)

Things can improve in your life much faster than you think.

Here is how Chrysler went zero to one.

Prioritize learning over comfort: He constantly left jobs he had already mastered to find harder challenges. He spent years traveling from town to town as a mechanic just to study different engines, and later took a 50% pay cut to jump from the established locomotive business into the raw, unproven automobile industry.

Obsessive self-education: To understand the new technology of automobiles, he went into massive debt to buy a $5,000 luxury car. Instead of driving it, he spent months completely dismantling and rebuilding it until he understood every single mechanical function.

Cross-pollinate industries: Chrysler's ultimate value came from applying the strict cost tracking and precise assembly schedules he mastered in the mature railroad industry to the chaotic early auto industry, cutting car production times from four days to just fifteen minutes. (55:00)

Other great ideas:

Never lose your temper on paper.
As a quick tempered young superintendent Chrysler wrote an angry letter after a rebuke from the general master mechanic. 

His boss Mr. Hickey called him in and taught him the rule: file angry letters in a drawer for a few days to cool off before you read them again.

An emotional reply on paper (email, text, etc.) becomes a permanent record of a temporary feeling that can wreck your career. 

A mandatory cooling off period keeps you logical and protects every important relationship. (29:00)

Origin story of the most beautiful building in the NYC skyline.
Chrysler wanted his sons to have real responsibility once they settled in New York. 

Remembering a structure from Paris he told the architect to build something taller than the Eiffel Tower. 

Then he made his son start by scrubbing floors in the basement so he would understand the entire operation before he ever managed it. (1:05:00)

Maintain gratitude for your humble beginnings.
In the postscript his co author reveals that Chrysler frequently cried when he remembered his early days as a poor roving mechanic.

He wanted his story to inspire other lonely ambitious boys still trying to find their way. (1:07:00)
