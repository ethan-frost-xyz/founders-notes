Founders #60 Yvon Chouinard (Patagonia)

In 2016, JPMorgan offered Databricks $10M to bring the platform on-prem. At the time, the company was sub $1M ARR. Founder/CEO Ali Ghodsi decline. They still ignore the on-prem market despite massive demand and revenue opportunities. If they had broken this cloud-only vision, I am sure the company would not be growing at 55% y/y at a $4.8B runrate. As a public company where every quarter matters, shareholder pressure to capture on-prem would be immense as soon as there is a bad quarter.

So, what does this have to do with Patagonia?

Patagonia would have failed if it went public in its first 40 years.

In the 1970s, Patagonia (then Chouinard Equipment) phased out its best-selling product, pitons (metal spikes for climbing), because they realized the gear was permanently damaging rock faces. Pitons accounted for 70% of their business. A public company cannot kill its main revenue source for "moral reasons." Eventually their aluminum chocks (which didn’t damage the rocks) became a bigger business and greatly strengthened brand loyalty.

In the 1980s, Malinda Chouinard drove the implementation of high-quality on-site child care. It was expensive and showed no clear ROI on a spreadsheet, but it led to incredibly low turnover and a more "human" workplace. This would have been unacceptable to shareholders in the 1980s, especially since Patagonia was struggling financially at the time.

In 1996, upon realizing conventional cotton was one of the most toxic crops on earth, Patagonia switched 100% of its sportswear line to organic cotton in just 18 months. This tripled their material costs and forced them to reduce their product line from 91 styles to 66. Public markets would have demanded a slower, "phased" rollout to protect quarterly earnings. Chouinard insisted on the "all-in" approach because he refused to be complicit in environmental damage.

After raising the $4B Series L, Ali Ghodsi stated that “In 2021, a lot of my peers that took their companies public, then in 2022 investors demanded EBITDA, so they all did layoffs and they cut down on all the futuristic stuff, innovation, and new products… We are now growing twice the growth rate of those companies.” (source: @tbpn)
